About Project

So, our project is all about trying to get better at predicting when highways are going to need repairs. It's a big deal because, as you can imagine, it's super important to keep roads in good shape for safety and to save money on fixing them. The problem is, a lot of times, people just look at one set of data, like how rough the road is, which doesn't tell the whole story. We thought, "What if we combine different data sources?

That's where the "data fusion" comes in. We're taking data from the Highway Performance Monitoring System (HPMS) and the Freight Analysis Framework (FAF) - basically, info on road conditions and freight movement in the state of Alabama. We're using data from 2013 to 2022, and then we're going to use machine learning to build a model that can predict how the roads will deteriorate.

These features were selected for their relevance to pavement deterioration and freight movement patterns across the national highway system.

Year range: 2013 to 2022

The dataset was preprocessed to align measurements over time and enable time series modeling at a per-section level.

How it is useful

Data fusion in highway maintenance and deterioration prediction offers benefits to various stakeholders: transportation agencies can achieve proactive and cost-effective maintenance, governments and policymakers can make informed decisions about infrastructure investment, the public experiences improved safety and reduced disruptions, researchers advance the field of predictive maintenance, and the freight industry benefits from more efficient transportation networks.

Limitations

Currently, the machine learning model has been trained using data limited to select road segments, specifically Interstate highways within the state of Alabama. As such, its predictive accuracy is optimized for those regions and may not generalize to other states or rural/local road systems. Expansion to national datasets is a potential future enhancement.
